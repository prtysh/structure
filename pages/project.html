A Is For Another: A Dictionary of AI

This project 

This project focuses on a distinctive aspect of AI: that it is shaped in terms of the human. So while AI is about machine learning, cognition and robots, it is also about how we understand what it means to be human, how our notions of the human, and of the non-human, are changing over time and what our inter-relationships could be. We want to show that ‘intelligence’, ‘humans’, ‘machines’, ‘data’ and ‘mind’ exist differently across a variety of cosmologies, sciences, and practices. A Is For Another puts diverse scholarship, art and cultural work in conversation with each other to further this thinking.

Artificial Intelligence, ‘AI’, is a suite of technologies that includes machine learning, computer vision, reasoning, and natural language processing, among others. 

In 1987, a computer was considered ‘intelligent’ because it beat the world’s highest ranked chess player. Now, a computer program will be considered intelligent if it can drive a car. Machines are intelligent, we are told, because they can identify and respond to human emotions. Intelligence then becomes a moving target defined by tasks like perception, pattern recognition, language processing or prediction.

Metaphors and narratives serve as shorthand for navigating new technologies — and AI

exists in an awkward space of both technology and metaphor. If we perceive AI through popular culture — the beautiful, calculating fembot Eva in Ex Machina or the threatening synthetic intelligence Skynet in Terminator — then it is a fantasy. But AI’s component technologies are already here and employed in object recognition and language translation, determining many aspects of social, cultural and political life.

The history of science shows us that we use metaphors to describe something unfamiliar and new, and this becomes entangled in how we study it, thus shaping the artefact in terms of its metaphor. Phrases like ‘information just wants to be free’ or ‘data is the new oil’ have shaped what we think digital technologies are. Such metaphors determine how a technology is developed, built, and will be regulated (or not regulated). For example, if, you think of AI as a child, or as a non-human species like an octopus, then how will you regulate it?

A Is For Another is a response to the world-making and culturally homogenous approaches to AI emerging from places like Silicon Valley and Hollywood; and from histories of the field as it has developed through Cognitive Psychology, Cybernetics, Computer Science and Linguistics since the 1950s.

For example, in Hollywood cinema and the European/US/UK Science Fiction genre, robots convey an anxiety about the displacement or erasure of human beings. In Japan, however, robots are perceived with less anxiety; and ‘robot’ is only one word for one kind of complex, dramatic non-human/human being. Steven Brown’s Tokyo Cyberpunk explores Japanese visual cultures of posthumanism, a panoply of vivid characters in speculative socio-political scenarios that “explore new possibilities of becoming at the rhizomatic intersection of different forms of intelligence, corporeality, and data processing.”

Or consider that fungi and robots, both non-human entities, are equally subjects of the Posthumanities; Anna Tsing’s ethnography of Matsutake cultivation has been an inspirational text for many entries here. However, Alexander Weheliye and Sylvia Wynter have written very different histories of ‘non-humans’, and the category of ‘man’, through the lens of Transatlantic slavery, colonialism and colonial plantations. Similarly, futurisms- Afro-futurism, Gulf-futurism- are syntheses of geopolitics with pop-culture, and psychogeographies of bodies moving forwards and back in time, in space, both online and off. What imaginaries of automation, bodies, data and minds exist here? What happens when we read a history of intelligence from the perspective of Cognitive Psychology alongside a social-materialist inquiry into the women who were known as computers?

The purpose of this project is not to advance a new theoretical rationale for how AI could or should be designed. Nor does it describe how specific AI technologies like machine learning or computer vision work in the world, how they demonstrate bias, or how they might be used to address climate change. (To know about these ideas, visit another new dictionary, the A to Z of AI).

Instead, we want to create forks and distractions in the path of how AI is being imagined and produced in the world.



Maya Indira Ganesh. Berlin, April 2020
